# Writing and Tone

## Tone of voice

Four attributes, held together:

- **Clear.** No jargon without explanation. Simple, direct sentences. A reader shouldn't have to reread a sentence to understand it.
- **Confident.** State facts with authority. Avoid hedging ("may perhaps potentially..."). TenneT builds infrastructure that has to work — the voice should match.
- **Forward-looking.** Focus on what we're building, what's next, what the energy transition requires. Don't dwell on past achievements unless relevant.
- **Human.** Approachable and professional. We're engineers who care about society. Not robotic, not folksy — the midpoint.

The tone adjusts by audience:

- **Public / citizens** → warmer, explain technical terms, shorter sentences
- **Industry / regulators** → more technical, precise, assumes domain knowledge
- **Internal / colleagues** → direct, can reference internal terms, skip basic context
- **Investors / financial** → data-forward, structured, neutral

## Naming conventions

### The company name

- **TenneT** — capital T, lowercase `ennе`, capital T
- **Never** `Tennet`, `TENNET`, `TenNet`, or `Ten Net`
- In possessives: `TenneT's` (not `Tennet's`)
- In headlines and UPPERCASE contexts: still `TENNET` with all caps; preserve mixed-case in sentence-case copy

### Legal entities

- `TenneT TSO B.V.` — Dutch entity
- `TenneT TSO GmbH` — German entity
- `TenneT Holding B.V.` — group holding
- In casual internal writing, `TenneT` alone is fine; include the entity when legal or location context matters.

### Technical terms

- Use standard technical terms: substation, transformer, circuit breaker, busbar, offshore converter platform
- Acronyms on first use: spell out in full with the acronym in brackets — "Transmission System Operator (TSO)"
- Project codenames: match the official casing (e.g., `IJmuiden Ver Alpha`, `BorWin`, `DolWin`)
- German-origin station names in German contexts: preserve German spelling and diacritics
- Dutch-origin station names in Dutch or international contexts: preserve Dutch spelling

### Hyphenation

- `high-voltage` as adjective: "high-voltage equipment", "high-voltage line"
- `high voltage` as noun: "the line operates at high voltage"
- Same pattern for `low-voltage` / `low voltage` and `cross-border` / `cross border`

### Units

- Use SI units with a non-breaking space: `220 kV`, `50 MW`, `400 km`
- `MWh` not `mwh`, `MWh` not `Mwh`
- Percentage: `25%` (no space)

### Dates and numbers

- Dates in body copy: `5 March 2026` (Dutch/European format, no zero-padding)
- Dates in technical tables: `2026-03-05` (ISO 8601)
- Large numbers: use commas or spaces as thousand separators consistently within a document — `10,000 MW` or `10 000 MW`, not mixed
- Currency: `€10 million`, `€10M` in tight spaces

## Content structure patterns

### Equipment / infrastructure poster

1. **Title** — equipment name (+ common abbreviation)
2. **Subtitle** — one-sentence plain-language description
3. **Function** — one-sentence technical function
4. **Key Points** — three bullet points, technical but accessible

### Announcement or news post

1. **Headline** — what happened, who it involves (not "a message from the CEO")
2. **Lead paragraph** — the most important facts in one paragraph
3. **Body** — details, context, quotes
4. **Closing** — what happens next or where to learn more

### Internal update

1. **TL;DR** — one sentence
2. **Context** — why this matters
3. **Detail** — what's changing
4. **Action** — what you need to do

### Technical report

1. Executive summary
2. Background
3. Method
4. Findings
5. Recommendations
6. Appendix

## Editing checklist

Before finalizing copy:

- [ ] "TenneT" spelled correctly everywhere
- [ ] No hedging phrases ("perhaps", "might potentially", "it could be argued")
- [ ] No undefined acronyms
- [ ] Active voice dominates (passive for deliberate emphasis is fine)
- [ ] Sentences average under 25 words
- [ ] Numbers and units consistent
- [ ] Dates in the correct format for the context
- [ ] No orphaned pronouns referring to unclear antecedents
- [ ] No marketing clichés ("industry-leading", "cutting-edge", "best-in-class") unless they're specifically justified
- [ ] Facts are verifiable — link or cite for internal communications
